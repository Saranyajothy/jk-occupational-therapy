What we do

“Occupational therapy is a client-centred health profession concerned with promoting health and well-being through occupation. The primary goal of occupational therapy is to enable people to participate in activities of everyday life.”  World Federation of Occupational Therapists (WFOT)


What to expect when I meet an occupational therapist?


On the initial assessment, the occupational therapist will involve with you to know your medical history and will also find out how your illness, injury or disability is affecting you and your occupations - the everyday things we do to take care of ourselves and others; socialise and have fun; and work and contribute to our community/society.


When an occupational therapist assesses you they may do any of the following to help them identify how they can help you:

  • Get you to explain what has been happening in your life and the difficulties you are experiencing
  • Involve you in activities that will demonstrate the difficulties you are having doing everyday activities
  • Use formal tests and Occupational Therapy assessment
  • Do a home visit to see how you are managing with your everyday occupations at home
  • Other types of assessment, e.g. seating assessment, cognitive assessment, etc

When the assessment is complete the occupational therapist will work with you to identify goals that Occupational Therapy can help you achieve. They will listen to what is important to you and see if they can either, help restore your ability to do your everyday occupations, or find another way of achieving your goal.



Example of Occupational Therapy with an Adult

An man who requires his home to be adapted to suit his needs due a physical disability is referred to an Occupational Therapist. The occupational therapist will carry out a home visit to assess what changes are required to enable him to live independently in his own home and be able to engage fully in his daily occupations. The occupational therapist will work with him to make a plan for adapting his home and support him in implementing this plan.

Home Modifications

Ageing and health changes in our lives may mean we have difficulty in managing daily tasks at home, for example, showering, making meals, entering and leaving our homes. Advice from an occupational therapist regarding home modifications can allow you to gain more control over your daily life, allow you to live at home more safely and comfortably as your needs changes. JK OT services have proven expertise in the area of home modifications. JK OT Services enable people to access appropriate funding through local city and county council that people may be eligible for grants. Simple home modification can include a grab rail or bath seat. Some examples of low cost modifications include Grab rails, non-slip flooring, lever taps, step wedges, 2 nd bannister, painting on step edges

More complex home modifications might include
  • Installing access ramps and railings
  • Lowering kitchen countertop heights for Wheelchair accessible kitchen
  • Modifying a bathroom to install a level access shower
  • Wheelchair accessible bathroom
  • Widening doorways and external pathways
  • Stairlifts
  • Through floor lift
  • Safety and environment monitoring system.

Seating:

JK OT services have wide experience in providing seating and postural support to clients who require mobility, seating and postural needs. Among the most common solutions we provide include:

  • Lightweight wheelchairs for occasional use
  • Manual or electrically-operated chairs to ensure day-to-day independence
  • Positioning aids and cushions to maximise comfort and relieve pressure
  • Supportive, comfortable armchairs suitable for those with orthopedic or other problems
  • Guidance with night-time positioning to aid restful sleep
  • Training and support for staff and carers

The aim of an occupational therapy assessment is to identify your seating needs in light of any health conditions you may have, as well as environmental factors that need to be considered. This allows us make recommendations to reduce them and (if desired) arrange for any recommended changes to be made to restore the maximum degree of independence in the home.
